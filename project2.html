Thesis: Souls-like Topology

Quantitative Research on Level Shortcuts & Diorama Pacing

Project Introduction

This master's thesis focuses on establishing a "Golden Benchmark" for non-linear 3D level design, heavily inspired by the intricate map structures found in Souls-like games. It aims to decode the magic behind interconnectivity, verticality, and reward-driven exploration through the lens of graph theory.

My Contributions

  • Research & Methodology: Formulated mathematical models integrating directed edges and cognitive load to quantify spatial complexity.
  • Engine Implementation: Utilized the Dying Light Engine to build diorama test maps, validating theoretical topological structures in a playable 3D space.
  • Architectural Integration: Applied real-world architectural principles (spatial syntax, courtyard layering, scenic borrowing) to control sightlines and navigation.
  • Data Analysis: Documented the correlation between loop rates, shortcut unlock pacing, and player satisfaction.

Design Analysis

A key finding in this project is the "Extended Stability Factor," which addresses the limitations of 2D abstraction when designing vertically stacked 3D environments. By mapping out nodes (checkpoints) and edges (paths/shortcuts), I calculated the optimal topological depth required before a player experiences cognitive fatigue.

The practical application of this research allows for the systematic generation of levels that feel highly organic yet are meticulously structured to guide player movement and combat pacing naturally.
